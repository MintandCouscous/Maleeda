Manja

Hyderabad's answer to Haldi

Azhar's family sends the turmeric over first — mixed with sandalwood and rosewater — and that opens the evening at the house in Shadnagar. First gift exchanged between the two families, before anything else. He's getting the same treatment at his own place, same night, other side of the city.

The women of the house take turns working it into Zeenath's hands, face and feet, each with a quiet dua. One house rule after tonight: she doesn't step out again until Sunday.

Nikah

& the Jalwa

A Nikah is a contract, not a sacrament — a khutbah, a proposal, an acceptance, a signature. Tap through for the detail:

What happens in the khutbah?+

A short sermon, three verses long. One on two souls being of the same root, finding each other again. One on speaking plainly and truthfully — a marriage runs on exactly that. One on staying steady, decades in.

Why aren't they in the same room?+

His side, her side, seated apart — the contract passes between them through the Wali and two witnesses rather than face to face.

It all rests on one moment: Qubool Hai, said three times, out loud. Done — married on paper. Everything after is about bringing the two of them together in person.

What's actually in the contract?+

The Mehr — a gift Azhar owes Zeenath outright, hers to keep, no strings, written into the Nikahnama before either of them says a word. Hers alone. Not symbolic, not the family's to touch.

What actually happens at the Jalwa?+

Two customs, back to back, both Hyderabadi through and through. First, the Jilwa: Zeenath's relatives hold a cloth around her and lower it, revealing her to Azhar's side in full bridal dress for the first time.

Then the Arsi Mushaf: one shawl over both of them, a mirror held up with a Quran resting above it. First look at each other as husband and wife — through that mirror, same second. Still under the shawl, they read a few verses aloud to one another. First words as a married couple: scripture.

Small packets of Maleeda go home with guests after — crushed fried roti, ghee, sugar, cardamom. Sweet thanks for showing up.

Zehar
A note on the name

Zehar

Ze·har — portmanteau of Zeenath and Azhar, coined three months after they met. Also: the Urdu word for poison. Three months was precisely how long Azhar had to google this. He did not. He is now legally, spiritually, and logistically bound by it.

October 2021 — they met. By January, someone had already decided this needed a name. The word that arrived was Zehar. Whether this was foresight, a very good joke, or a soft warning dressed as romance, the record does not show.

What followed was four years of: the same shows watched in different cities at the same time, calls that outlasted dinner, and enough Air India bookings between them to have opinions about which route has the better cabin crew. Several telecom companies quietly flourished. Neither of them noticed.

Today they are in the same city, the same room, the same family. Azhar cannot say he wasn't warned. He can, and does, say it was entirely worth it.
